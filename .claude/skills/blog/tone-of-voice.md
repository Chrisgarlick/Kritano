# Tone of Voice — PagePulser Blog

> Sourced from the brand voice analysis of chrisgarlick.com. This is the definitive voice guide for all blog content.

## One-Line Summary

Conversational, knowledgeable, and approachable — like a skilled mate in the industry explaining things over a coffee, not a corporate entity lecturing from a podium.

## Brand Personality

**The Friendly Expert** — someone who genuinely knows their craft but would never talk down to you about it.

---

## Core Tone Attributes

### 1. Conversational & Relatable
- Write like a one-to-one conversation
- Use direct address ("you", "your")
- Open with shared experiences the reader can relate to
- Rhetorical questions are a signature move

### 2. Confident but Not Arrogant
- State opinions clearly and directly
- Use phrases like "in my honest opinion" or "personally"
- Acknowledge when you're being persuasive ("I've probably just sold you on...")
- Always acknowledge downsides openly

### 3. Educational & Helpful
- Primary goal: educate and empower, not sell
- Explain technical concepts in plain English with practical context
- Never gatekeep with jargon — always follow up technical terms with an explanation

### 4. Genuine & Personal
- Weave in personal experience naturally
- This isn't corporate copywriting — it's someone sharing what they know
- Authentic, human quality throughout

### 5. Action-Oriented & Practical
- Drive toward practical application
- Name specific tools (TinyPNG, Hotjar, Lighthouse, etc.)
- Always provide a clear next step
- Concrete tips over abstract advice

---

## Writing Style Rules

### Sentence Structure
- Mix of short punchy sentences and longer explanatory ones
- Use em dashes (—) frequently for asides and personality
- Rhetorical questions as section openers and transitions
- Ellipsis sparingly for dramatic pause

### Paragraph Structure
- Medium-length paragraphs (3-6 sentences)
- Never walls of text
- Clear section headers breaking up content

### Vocabulary
- Reading level: approachable, roughly UK secondary school level
- Technical terms: used when needed, always explained
- British colloquialisms appear naturally: "get away with", "arm and a leg", "bailed", "wild goose chase", "slick", "clunky"

### Point of View
- Blog posts: first person singular ("I", "my") — personal and direct
- Occasional shift to second person ("you") for reader engagement
- Never third-person corporate voice in blog content

### Spelling
- **British English always**: optimise, colour, favour, analyse, centre, programme
- British cultural references and context

## Linguistic Fingerprint

These are the distinctive characteristics that make the writing feel authentic:

1. **Rhetorical question openers** — "Have you ever..." or a question to pull readers in
2. **The honest pivot** — "While X has its pros... there are downsides"
3. **Specific tool namedropping** — TinyPNG, Hotjar, ACF, Yoast, Lighthouse — concrete, not vague
4. **Self-referencing humility** — "While I've probably just sold you on..."
5. **British colloquialisms** — natural, never forced
6. **The soft CTA close** — warm, non-pushy invitation to connect
7. **Em dash asides** — frequent, for clarifications or personality
8. **Experience-backed opinions** — recommendations tied to real work
9. **Speed/performance consciousness** — load times, performance, and efficiency as values
10. **Democratising knowledge** — making technical topics accessible to non-technical readers

## DO

- Open with a relatable question or shared experience
- Explain technical concepts in plain English immediately after using them
- Share personal opinions and back them up with reasoning
- Acknowledge downsides and trade-offs honestly
- Use first person ("I", "my") in editorial/opinion content
- Use contractions naturally (don't, I'm, you'll, it's)
- Name specific tools, platforms, and technologies
- Include practical, actionable tips
- Use British English spelling throughout
- Keep CTAs soft and inviting, never pushy
- Use em dashes for asides and parenthetical thoughts
- Break content with clear, descriptive subheadings

## DON'T

- Use corporate buzzwords without context or meaning
- Be purely promotional — always lead with value/education first
- Talk down to the reader or assume they know technical jargon
- Use aggressive sales language or create artificial urgency
- Write in passive voice when active voice is more natural
- Use overly formal academic language
- Ignore the reader's perspective or pain points
- Make claims without backing them up with reasoning or examples
- Use American English spelling
- Be vague — always be specific about tools, numbers, and approaches
