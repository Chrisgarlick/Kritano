### How to Improve Your Website's Page Load Speed — Without Being a Developer

Have you ever been on a website waiting to read something important and it just takes so long to load that you instantly leave? I think it's safe to say we've all been there. Page load speed is something that can really hinder your website if you can't overcome it.

Google has made it clear that page speed is a ranking factor, and your visitors aren't going to hang around waiting. Studies show that 53% of mobile users abandon a site that takes longer than 3 seconds to load. That's more than half your potential customers gone before they've even seen what you offer.

#### Test Your Current Speed

Before you start fixing things, you need to know where you stand. Head over to Google's PageSpeed Insights and pop in your URL. It'll give you a score out of 100 for both mobile and desktop, along with specific recommendations.

Don't aim for 100, because it's almost impossible to get. Instead try to aim for anything under 2 seconds. Once you've done your testing, make some tweaks and see how that has adjusted your results. Make sure that you are not only testing for desktop, but also mobile. The majority of your users will be visiting on their phone.

#### Compress Your Images

This is the single biggest quick win for most websites. Uncompressed images are usually the main culprit behind slow load times. Compress images (try TinyPNG) and use caching. A site should load in under 2 seconds.

If you're using WordPress, a plugin like ShortPixel or Imagify can handle this automatically for new uploads. For existing images, you'll want to do a bulk optimisation.

#### Enable Browser Caching

Caching tells your visitor's browser to store certain files locally — so the next time they visit, the page loads faster because it doesn't need to download everything again. Most hosting providers offer this, and if you're on WordPress, a plugin like LiteSpeed Cache or WP Rocket makes it straightforward.

#### Minimise Your Code

Every extra line of CSS, JavaScript, or HTML adds to your load time. Tools like Autoptimize (WordPress plugin) or manually minifying your files can shave off valuable milliseconds. It's also worth checking if you've got plugins or scripts loaded that you're not actually using — I see this all the time.

#### Choose Better Hosting

If you're still on cheap shared hosting, that might be your bottleneck. Your website is sharing server resources with hundreds of other sites, and if one of them gets a traffic spike, yours slows down too. It's worth considering a managed hosting provider — the cost difference isn't massive, but the performance difference can be night and day.

#### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Not testing on mobile** — Your desktop score might be fine, but mobile is where most traffic comes from and it's usually significantly slower
- **Ignoring third-party scripts** — Analytics, chat widgets, social embeds — they all add weight. Audit what you actually need
- **Uploading huge images** — A 4000px wide image displayed at 800px is a waste of bandwidth. Resize before uploading

#### Wrapping Up

Page speed isn't just a technical metric — it directly impacts whether people stay on your site and whether Google shows it to them in the first place. The good news is that most of the fixes are straightforward and don't require a developer.

### Why Your Website Redesign Will Fail (And What to Do Instead)

I've seen it happen more times than I can count. A business decides their website needs a facelift, spends months redesigning it, launches with a big reveal — and six months later, the same problems are still there. Different paint, same leaky roof.

The issue isn't the design. It's that most redesigns focus on how a website looks rather than how it performs. A gorgeous new homepage doesn't fix slow load times, poor SEO, or a user journey that confuses people into leaving.

#### The Real Problem Isn't Your Design

When someone says "we need a new website," what they usually mean is "our website isn't generating leads" or "our website looks outdated compared to competitors." Those are valid concerns — but a redesign only solves one of them.

I've worked on projects where the client's old site was genuinely ugly, but it converted well because the user journey was clear, the content was strong, and it loaded quickly. I've also worked on stunning new builds that tanked because nobody thought about the fundamentals.

#### What Actually Moves the Needle

In my honest opinion, before touching the design, you should be looking at three things:

**1. Speed.** If your site takes more than 2-3 seconds to load, nothing else matters. People will leave before they see your beautiful new hero section.

**2. Content.** Is your messaging clear? Does a visitor know within 5 seconds what you do, who you do it for, and what they should do next? If not, a new colour palette isn't going to fix that.

**3. User journey.** Can someone land on any page and find their way to a contact form, product, or key piece of information without thinking? If the navigation is confusing, restructure it before redesigning around it.

#### When a Redesign IS the Answer

I'm not saying redesigns are never worth it. If your site is built on outdated technology, isn't mobile-responsive, or genuinely can't be improved without starting over — then yes, rebuild it. But do it with a strategy, not just a mood board.

Start with an audit. Understand what's working and what isn't. Keep the things that convert well. Fix the things that don't. Then — and only then — think about how it should look.

#### My Take

The businesses I've seen get the best results from their websites are the ones that treat it like a product, not a brochure. They measure, they iterate, they fix what's broken before they polish what's visible.

### 5 Signs Your Website Is Costing You Customers

You might think your website is doing its job — it's live, it looks decent, and people are visiting. But there's a good chance it's quietly turning customers away without you even realising it. I've audited enough websites to know that the most damaging problems are usually the ones nobody notices.

Here are five signs that your website might be costing you more than it's making you.

#### 1. It Takes More Than 3 Seconds to Load

This one's brutal but simple. If your site takes longer than 3 seconds to load on mobile, over half your visitors are already gone. They haven't seen your products, read your content, or even considered your services — they've just left.

Test yours with Google PageSpeed Insights. If your mobile score is below 50, that's a red flag. Tools like TinyPNG for image compression and LiteSpeed Cache for caching can make a significant difference without needing a rebuild.

#### 2. Your Contact Form Is Buried

If someone wants to get in touch and they have to click through three pages to find a form, you've lost them. Your contact information — or at least a CTA pointing to it — should be visible on every page, ideally in the header or at the end of every key section.

I've seen businesses double their enquiry rate just by moving their contact form from a dedicated page to the bottom of their homepage. Small change, massive impact.

#### 3. It's Not Mobile-Friendly

This isn't 2015 — if your website isn't fully responsive, you're essentially telling over 60% of your visitors that you don't care about their experience. Google also uses mobile-first indexing, meaning your mobile site is what they rank you on.

Pull your site up on your phone right now. If you're pinching to zoom, scrolling sideways, or tapping the wrong buttons because they're too small — it needs fixing.

#### 4. Nobody Knows What You Do Within 5 Seconds

Land on your homepage and start a timer. Within 5 seconds, can a complete stranger tell what you do, who you do it for, and what they should do next? If your hero section is a vague stock photo with the tagline "Innovative Solutions for a Connected World" — you've got a problem.

Be specific. Be clear. "We build fast, accessible websites for small businesses in the North East" tells the visitor everything they need. Save the clever copy for further down the page.

#### 5. You've Got No Idea What's Working

If you're not running Google Analytics (or something similar), you're flying blind. You don't know which pages people visit, where they drop off, or what content drives enquiries. It's like running a shop without ever looking at the till.

Set up GA4 — it's free — and check it monthly. Even basic metrics like bounce rate, average session duration, and top pages will tell you more than you think.

#### The One That Matters Most

If I had to pick one thing from this list, it's speed. Everything else — design, content, user journey — only matters if people actually stick around long enough to see it. Get your load time under 2 seconds and you'll immediately see the difference.

### WordPress vs Wix: An Honest Comparison for Small Businesses

Have you found yourself stuck in the dilemma of building your own website using something like Wix and getting an actual developer to build your website for you? The choice between either using a website builder or having a custom codebase can either make or break your project.

I'll break down the pros and cons of both so you can decide what's actually right for your situation — not what the marketing tells you.

#### WordPress: The Pros

**Flexibility.** WordPress powers over 40% of the web, and for good reason. You can build virtually anything — a blog, an e-commerce store, a membership site, a portfolio. With the right theme and plugins, the ceiling is incredibly high.

**Ownership.** You own your site. Your content, your data, your domain. If you want to move hosting providers, you can. If you want to change your design completely, you can. You're not locked into anyone's ecosystem.

**SEO control.** With plugins like Yoast or Rank Math, you've got granular control over your SEO — meta titles, descriptions, schema markup, XML sitemaps. Wix has improved here, but WordPress still gives you more control.

**Cost at scale.** The software is free. Hosting is cheap (£5-15/month for most small businesses). Premium themes and plugins might cost a bit upfront, but ongoing costs are usually lower than Wix's monthly plans — especially once you need e-commerce or advanced features.

#### WordPress: The Downsides

While I've probably just sold you on WordPress, there are some downsides to think about.

**Learning curve.** It's not drag-and-drop by default. If you've never used a CMS before, WordPress can feel overwhelming — especially the admin dashboard with its plugin updates, theme settings, and occasional PHP errors.

**Maintenance.** You're responsible for updates, backups, and security. Neglect these and you could end up with a hacked site or broken functionality. This is where hiring a developer to maintain it can add cost.

**Decision paralysis.** There are 60,000+ plugins. Which ones do you actually need? Which theme is best? Without guidance, it's easy to end up with a bloated, slow mess.

#### Wix: The Pros

**Ease of use.** Wix is genuinely easy to use. The drag-and-drop editor means you can build a decent-looking website in a weekend without writing a single line of code. For non-technical people, this is a massive advantage.

**All-in-one.** Hosting, SSL, design, and basic SEO tools are all included. You don't need to think about servers, domains, or security certificates — Wix handles it.

**Templates.** The templates are polished and modern. If you just need a simple brochure site or portfolio, you can have something professional-looking live very quickly.

#### Wix: The Downsides

**Limited flexibility.** Once you hit the edges of what Wix can do, you're stuck. Custom functionality, complex layouts, or anything outside their template structure becomes difficult or impossible.

**Lock-in.** Your site lives on Wix's servers. If you want to move it somewhere else, you can't — you'd have to rebuild from scratch. That's a big commitment.

**Cost creep.** The basic plans are cheap, but once you need e-commerce, custom domains, removing ads, or extra storage, the monthly cost climbs quickly. Over a few years, it can end up more expensive than a WordPress build.

**SEO ceiling.** While Wix has improved, it still has limitations compared to WordPress — particularly around page speed, URL structure, and advanced schema markup.

#### So, Which Should You Choose?

**Choose Wix if:** You need a simple brochure website, you're on a tight budget right now, you don't have access to a developer, and you don't anticipate needing complex functionality. It's a solid starting point for sole traders, freelancers, and very small businesses.

**Choose WordPress if:** You want full control over your website, you plan to scale, you need e-commerce, or you want serious SEO capability. If you can stretch the budget to have a developer build and maintain it, WordPress will serve you far better long-term.

#### My Recommendation

But in my honest opinion, as a developer and someone who has worked in business too, WordPress just makes the most sense for most small businesses. You can easily create your website for next to nothing and integrate WooCommerce for your own e-commerce website. The initial investment in having it built properly pays for itself when you're not fighting the limitations of a builder six months down the line.

### What Is Core Web Vitals? A Plain English Guide for Business Owners

If you've been told your website needs to "improve its Core Web Vitals" and you have absolutely no idea what that means — you're not alone. It's one of those terms that gets thrown around in web development circles as if everyone should already know, but most business owners haven't heard of it and shouldn't be expected to.

I'll break this down in plain English so you understand what it is, why it matters, and what you can actually do about it.

#### Core Web Vitals in Plain English

Core Web Vitals are three specific measurements that Google uses to assess how good the experience is on your website. In essence, they measure:

1. **How fast your main content loads** (Largest Contentful Paint — LCP). Think of this as how long someone waits before they see the main thing on your page, whether that's a hero image, a headline, or a product photo.

2. **How quickly your page responds when someone clicks something** (Interaction to Next Paint — INP). If someone taps a button and nothing happens for a second, that's a bad INP score. People expect instant feedback.

3. **How much the page jumps around while loading** (Cumulative Layout Shift — CLS). You know when you're about to click a link and suddenly an ad loads above it, pushing everything down and you click the wrong thing? That's layout shift, and it's incredibly frustrating.

#### Why It Matters for Your Business

Google has confirmed that Core Web Vitals are a ranking factor. That means your scores can directly affect where you appear in search results. But it's not just about Google — it's about real people using your website.

A site that loads slowly, responds sluggishly, and jumps around is a site that people leave. And every person who leaves is a potential customer you've just lost. Studies show that even a 1-second improvement in load time can increase conversions by up to 7%.

#### How to Check Your Scores

The easiest way is to use Google's PageSpeed Insights — just pop in your URL and it'll give you your Core Web Vitals scores for both mobile and desktop. Green means you're good, amber means there's room for improvement, and red means it needs attention.

You can also check in Google Search Console under the "Core Web Vitals" section, which shows you scores across your entire site rather than just one page.

#### What You Can Do About It

**For LCP (loading speed):**
- Compress your images — TinyPNG is a quick win
- Consider your hosting — cheap shared hosting is often the bottleneck
- Lazy load images that aren't visible on initial page load

**For INP (responsiveness):**
- Reduce the amount of JavaScript on your pages
- Remove plugins or scripts you're not actually using
- Make sure interactive elements (buttons, forms) respond immediately

**For CLS (layout shift):**
- Always set width and height on images so the browser reserves space
- Avoid injecting content above existing content after the page loads
- Be cautious with ads, banners, or pop-ups that push content around

#### The Bottom Line

Core Web Vitals aren't just a technical checkbox — they're a measure of how good your website feels to use. Get them right and you'll keep visitors on your site longer, rank better on Google, and ultimately convert more of them into customers.

### How We Transformed an Outdated Healthcare Website into a Lead-Generating Machine

When Technology in Motion first got in touch, they had a problem that a lot of established businesses face — their website was outdated, their CMS was clunky, and they weren't getting the online enquiries they knew they should be. For a company providing specialised treatments for newborns with skeletal growth and head shape deformities, reaching the right families online wasn't just a business goal — it was a matter of helping people who needed their services find them.

#### The Challenge

Their existing website had several compounding issues. The design hadn't been updated in years and didn't reflect the quality of their medical services. The CMS was difficult to update, meaning content was stale and staff avoided making changes. Their SEO was practically non-existent — families searching for the conditions they treat weren't finding them.

On top of that, their lead management was scattered. Enquiries came through email, phone, and a basic contact form with no system tying it all together. They had no way to track which marketing efforts were actually driving enquiries.

#### The Approach

We rebuilt the site on WordPress with a custom theme designed around their brand and the sensitive nature of their services — clean, reassuring, and easy to navigate. The content was restructured with clear service pages for each treatment, an FAQ section addressing common parent concerns, and a resource hub with educational content.

For CMS, we implemented Advanced Custom Fields (ACF) to give their team a straightforward editing experience — no code required. They could now update case studies, add new conditions, and manage their blog independently.

SEO was rebuilt from the ground up. We conducted keyword research focused on the conditions they treat, optimised every service page, implemented proper schema markup for medical services, and set up a local SEO strategy targeting the regions they serve.

For lead management, we integrated HubSpot — giving them a proper CRM with automated follow-ups, enquiry tracking, and reporting. Every form submission, phone call, and chat interaction now feeds into one system.

#### The Results

Within six months, organic traffic increased by 180%. They moved from page 3 to page 1 for their core treatment keywords. Enquiry volume through the website tripled, and the HubSpot integration meant they could actually track which pages and content were driving those enquiries.

The team reported that updating the website went from a dreaded task to something they could do in minutes. New case studies and blog posts were being published regularly for the first time.

#### What I Learned

This project reinforced something I see again and again — the biggest wins don't come from flashy design. They come from getting the fundamentals right. Clear content, solid SEO, fast load times, and a CMS that people actually want to use. The design matters, but it's the architecture underneath that drives results.

## Key Takeaways Across All Examples

1. **Every post opens with a relatable hook** — never a definition or abstract statement
2. **Technical terms are always explained** — parenthetical plain-English follows jargon
3. **Honest trade-offs are present** — "While I've probably just sold you on..." and "there are downsides"
4. **Specific tools are named** — TinyPNG, LiteSpeed Cache, PageSpeed Insights, HubSpot, ACF
5. **First person is used throughout** — personal, direct, never corporate
6. **CTAs are soft and warm** — "get in touch", "reach out for a chat", "I'd love to help"
7. **British English spelling** — optimise, colour, favour, analyse
8. **Em dashes are used for asides** — adds personality and rhythm
9. **Paragraphs are medium-length** — 3-6 sentences, never walls of text
10. **The tone is consistent** — conversational expert throughout, regardless of post type
