# Brand Voice Analysis: chrisgarlick.com

> Deep-dive analysis of the tone of voice, writing style, and communication patterns used across chrisgarlick.com. Derived from comprehensive scanning of all pages including homepage, about, contact, 5 case studies, and 4 blog posts.

---

## 1. Voice Overview

**One-line summary:** Conversational, knowledgeable, and approachable — like a skilled mate in the industry explaining things over a coffee, not a corporate entity lecturing from a podium.

**Brand personality archetype:** The Friendly Expert — someone who genuinely knows their craft but would never talk down to you about it.

---

## 2. Core Tone Attributes

### 2.1 Conversational & Relatable
The writing consistently feels like a one-to-one conversation. It uses direct address ("you", "your") and frequently opens with shared experiences the reader can relate to.

**Examples:**
- "Have you ever been on a website waiting to read something important and it just takes so long to load that you instantly leave? I think it's safe to say we've all been there."
- "Have you ever landed on a website that looked slick but left you confused, clicking around aimlessly? Or worse, one that felt so clunky you bailed in seconds? I've been there, and I bet you have too."
- "Have you found yourself stuck in the dilemma of building your own website using something like Wix and getting an actual developer to build your website for you?"

**Pattern:** Blog posts almost always open with a relatable question or scenario that pulls the reader in through shared experience.

### 2.2 Confident but Not Arrogant
Opinions are stated clearly and directly, often with phrases like "in my honest opinion" or "personally", making them feel like genuine advice rather than hard sells.

**Examples:**
- "But in my honest opinion, as a developer and someone who has worked in business too, Wordpress just makes the most sense."
- "Personally, If you don't have time for a full blown Laravel build, I would suggest a developer builds you one using Wordpress"
- "While I've probably just sold you on getting a custom coded website, there is definitely some downsides."

**Pattern:** Self-awareness about persuasion ("I've probably just sold you on...") builds trust. Acknowledges downsides openly rather than being purely promotional.

### 2.3 Educational & Helpful
The primary goal of content is to educate and empower the reader, not just to sell services. Technical concepts are explained in plain English with practical context.

**Examples:**
- "UI (user interface) is how your site looks and feels—think colors, buttons, layouts. UX (user experience) is how it guides users to take action, like signing up or buying."
- "Inside your performance metrics you can easily see how your website renders, including the largest contentful paint and the first contentful paint, in essence, this is usually the speed of the render for your hero section"
- "Since it's open source there are thousands of developers who are constantly building plugins to enhance the CMS."

**Pattern:** Technical jargon is always followed by a plain-English explanation or analogy. Never assumes the reader knows the terminology.

### 2.4 Genuine & Personal
The writing has an authentic, human quality. Personal journey and experiences are woven in naturally — this isn't corporate copywriting, it's someone sharing what they know.

**Examples:**
- "What started as a simple curiosity soon turned into a deep fascination with programming."
- "It was my developer on the Alldio team who encouraged me to start learning Python, and that advice was a turning point in my journey."
- "Web development is more than just a career for me—it's a continuous learning experience that challenges me to grow and improve every day."

### 2.5 Action-Oriented & Practical
Content consistently drives toward practical application. Tips are concrete, tools are named specifically, and there's always a clear next step.

**Examples:**
- "Compress images (try TinyPNG) and use caching. A site should load in under 2 seconds"
- "Don't aim for 100, because it's almost impossible to get. Instead try to aim for anything under 2 seconds."
- "If you're wanting a website audit to see how I can help improve your website, please get in touch"

## 3. Writing Style Patterns

### 3.1 Sentence Structure
- **Mix of short and long sentences.** Short punchy sentences for emphasis, longer ones for explanation.
- Uses em dashes (—) frequently to add asides and parenthetical thoughts mid-sentence.
- Rhetorical questions are used heavily as section openers and transitions.
- Ellipsis used sparingly but effectively for dramatic pause ("upwards of $300 a month, with their most expensive being almost $5000 per month...")

### 3.2 Paragraph Structure
- **Blog posts:** Medium-length paragraphs (3-6 sentences). Never walls of text.
- **Case studies:** Longer, more formal paragraphs — these read more professionally than the blog posts.
- **About page:** Story-driven, chronological paragraphs with clear section headers.

### 3.3 Vocabulary Level
- **Reading level:** Approachable, roughly UK secondary school level. No unnecessarily complex words.
- **Technical terms:** Used when needed but always explained. Never gatekeeping with jargon.
- **Colloquialisms:** British English colloquialisms appear naturally — "get away with", "arm and a leg", "a mate", "bailed", "wild goose chase", "slick", "clunky".

### 3.4 Point of View
- **Blog posts:** First person singular ("I", "my") — personal and direct.
- **Case studies:** Third person when referring to the agency ("Coffee implemented..."), shifting to first person plural occasionally ("We also created solution pages").
- **About page:** First person singular throughout — deeply personal narrative.
- **Homepage:** Mix of first person ("I'm a self-taught developer") and second person ("let's bring your vision to life").

### 3.5 British English
- Consistent use of British English spelling: "optimise" (not optimize), "colours" (not colors), "favour" (not favor).
- British cultural references and pricing in context of UK market.

## 4. Content Structure Patterns

### 4.1 Blog Post Formula
1. **Hook:** Relatable question or scenario the reader has experienced
2. **Context:** Brief explanation of why this topic matters (2-3 sentences)
3. **Body:** Practical breakdown with named tools, platforms, and specific examples
4. **Honest opinion:** Personal recommendation with reasoning
5. **CTA:** Soft sell — "get in touch", "check out my portfolio", "fill in the form"

### 4.2 Case Study Formula
1. **Client name + one-line value proposition** as headline
2. **Tags/labels** for services used (SEO, HubSpot, Shopify, etc.)
3. **Introduction:** Sets the scene — who the client is and what they needed
4. **The Challenge:** Detailed breakdown of problems (uses numbered/structured lists)
5. **The Solution:** What was implemented and why
6. **The Results:** Measurable outcomes and positive impact
7. **Related Work:** Links to other case studies

### 4.3 Heading Style
- **Blog posts:** Conversational, sometimes question-based ("Should you use a website builder?", "What is the best option?")
- **Case studies:** More formal and descriptive ("Addressing a Niche Market with Precision", "Strategic HubSpot and WooCommerce Integration")
- Subheadings use title case

## 7. Voice Do's and Don'ts

### DO:
- Open with a relatable question or shared experience
- Explain technical concepts in plain English immediately after using them
- Share personal opinions and back them up with reasoning
- Acknowledge downsides and trade-offs honestly
- Use first person ("I", "my") in editorial/opinion content
- Use contractions naturally (don't, I'm, you'll, it's)
- Name specific tools, platforms, and technologies
- Include practical, actionable tips
- Use British English spelling throughout
- Keep CTAs soft and inviting, never pushy
- Use em dashes for asides and parenthetical thoughts
- Break content with clear, descriptive subheadings

### DON'T:
- Use corporate buzzwords without context or meaning
- Be purely promotional — always lead with value/education first
- Talk down to the reader or assume they know technical jargon
- Use aggressive sales language or create artificial urgency
- Write in passive voice when active voice is more natural
- Use overly formal academic language
- Ignore the reader's perspective or pain points
- Make claims without backing them up with reasoning or examples
- Use American English spelling
- Be vague — always be specific about tools, numbers, and approaches

## 9. Narrative Themes

### 9.1 The Self-Taught Journey
A recurring narrative thread is the self-taught origin story — from curiosity to professional developer. This grounds the brand in authenticity and relatability.

### 9.2 Honest Comparisons
Content frequently compares options (CMS platforms, builders vs code, etc.) with genuine pros and cons for each. The voice never dismisses options entirely but gives honest assessments.

### 9.3 Practical Over Theoretical
Every piece of content ties back to practical application. Even when discussing concepts like UX theory, it's framed through real examples and actionable steps.

### 9.4 Client Success Through Partnership
Case studies frame the relationship as collaborative ("Coffee was engaged to leverage its expertise", "Close collaboration with the design department"). The client isn't being "done to" — they're being partnered with.

## 10. Sample Voice Guidelines for New Content

### Writing a blog post in this voice:
```
1. Start with a relatable question the reader has experienced
2. Briefly explain why this topic matters (2-3 sentences)
3. Break into clear sections with conversational subheadings
4. For each point: explain the concept, give a real example, share your opinion
5. Be honest about trade-offs — "While X is great, here's the downside..."
6. End with a personal recommendation and soft CTA
7. Tone: like explaining something to a friend who's smart but not technical
```

### Writing a case study in this voice:
```
1. Lead with the client name and a clear value-proposition headline
2. Set the scene: who the client is, what industry, what they needed
3. Detail the challenges with specificity (not vague "they needed growth")
4. Explain the solution methodically — name every tool and platform
5. Share results with tangible outcomes
6. Keep tone professional but not stiff — still human underneath
7. Link to related work at the end
```

### Writing homepage/service copy in this voice:
```
1. Lead with what you do and who you are — be direct
2. Use first person to create personal connection
3. Highlight passion and purpose, not just skills
4. CTAs should feel like invitations, not demands
5. Keep it concise — every word should earn its place
```

## 11. Linguistic Fingerprint

These are the subtle, distinctive characteristics that make the writing feel uniquely "Chris Garlick":

1. **Rhetorical question openers** — Nearly every blog post starts with "Have you ever..." or a question
2. **The honest pivot** — "While X has its pros... there are downsides" (balanced takes)
3. **Specific tool namedropping** — TinyPNG, Hotjar, ACF, Yoast, Litespeed Cache — concrete, not vague
4. **Self-referencing humility** — "While I've probably just sold you on..."
5. **British colloquialisms** — "arm and a leg", "bailed", "get away with", "wild goose chase"
6. **The soft CTA close** — Always ends content with a warm, non-pushy invitation to connect
7. **Em dash asides** — Frequent use of em dashes to slip in clarifications or personality
8. **Experience-backed opinions** — "at Coffee Design, we often opt for..." — recommendations tied to real work
9. **Speed/performance consciousness** — Frequently references load times, performance, and efficiency as values
10. **Democratising knowledge** — Consistent effort to make technical topics accessible to non-technical readers
