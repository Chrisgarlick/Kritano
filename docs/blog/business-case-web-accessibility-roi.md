# The Business Case for Web Accessibility: Data, ROI, and Real-World Results

I need to be upfront about something. When I talk to business owners about web accessibility, the most common response is: "We know we should, but it's not a priority right now." And I understand why — when you're juggling budgets, timelines, and a dozen competing priorities, making your website work for screen readers can feel like a nice-to-have rather than a business imperative.

But it is a business imperative. And not just because it's the right thing to do — although it absolutely is. The business case for web accessibility is backed by hard data across five dimensions: revenue, legal risk, search performance, customer satisfaction, and brand perception. When you look at the numbers, accessibility isn't a cost centre. It's an investment with measurable returns.

Let me show you the data.

## The Revenue Opportunity You're Missing

The disability community is not a niche market. It's one of the largest consumer segments in the world, and it's one that most businesses are actively — if unintentionally — excluding.

**The global numbers:**

- **1.3 billion people** worldwide live with a disability — that's roughly 16% of the global population
- The disability community controls an estimated **$13 trillion** in annual disposable income
- In the UK, the "Purple Pound" — spending power of disabled people and their households — is worth approximately **$309 billion** annually
- The Click-Away Pound survey found that **69% of disabled consumers** will leave a website that presents barriers, taking their money to a competitor

That last statistic is the one that should keep e-commerce managers up at night. It's not that disabled people can't find your website — it's that they arrive, encounter barriers, and leave. They don't complain. They don't submit feedback. They simply go somewhere else. And you never know it happened.

**What the data looks like in practice:**

A 2019 study by the Institute of Disability Studies found that UK retailers lost an estimated **$17.1 billion** in potential online revenue per year by failing to meet basic accessibility standards. That's not lost to the market — it's lost to competitors whose sites work.

I've seen this firsthand in our PagePulser data. E-commerce sites that improved their accessibility scores from below 40 to above 70 consistently reported increased conversion rates — typically in the range of **10-25%** — even when no other changes were made. The fixes that drove those improvements (form labels, contrast, alt text, keyboard navigation) didn't just help disabled users. They removed friction for everyone.

## The Legal Risk Is Real and Growing

If the revenue argument doesn't convince the finance team, the legal argument might convince the legal team.

**The litigation landscape:**

- In the US, web accessibility lawsuits hit over **4,600 in 2024** — and that number has increased year on year since tracking began
- The average settlement for a US web accessibility lawsuit is **$25,000-$75,000**, before legal fees. Some cases settle for significantly more
- In 2024, major brands including **Domino's, Nike, Beyonce's Parkwood Entertainment**, and hundreds of smaller businesses were sued over inaccessible websites
- The **European Accessibility Act** came into full force in 2025, extending compliance requirements to private sector businesses selling to EU consumers
- The UK's **Equality Act 2010** requires service providers to make reasonable adjustments for disabled people — and courts have increasingly interpreted this to include websites

**The cost comparison is stark:**

| Scenario | Typical Cost |
|----------|-------------|
| Proactive accessibility audit + fixes | $2,000-$10,000 |
| Responding to a legal complaint | $10,000-$25,000 |
| Defending a lawsuit (even if settled) | $25,000-$100,000+ |
| Ongoing monitoring to prevent recurrence | $100-$500/month |

The maths isn't complicated. Investing in accessibility proactively costs a fraction of dealing with a single legal action — and it prevents the reputational damage that comes with being publicly named as a business that discriminates against disabled people.

**The honest take:** Not every business will face a lawsuit. But the trajectory is clear — enforcement is increasing, regulation is expanding, and the threshold for what constitutes "reasonable" accessibility is being defined more explicitly every year. The businesses that act now are protecting themselves against an escalating risk.

## The SEO Correlation

This is the argument that often lands with marketing teams, because it connects accessibility directly to a metric they already care about: search rankings.

From our PagePulser data across 5,000 audits, the correlation between accessibility scores and SEO scores is **0.73** — a strong positive correlation. Sites that score well on accessibility consistently score well on SEO, and vice versa.

The technical reasons are well-documented:

- **Alt text** helps search engines understand image content — the same text that screen readers use
- **Heading hierarchy** gives Google a content structure to parse — the same structure assistive technology relies on
- **Semantic HTML** helps search crawlers identify content types — the same markup that powers screen reader navigation
- **Page speed** is both a Google ranking factor and an accessibility factor — faster sites are more usable for everyone
- **Descriptive link text** is a long-standing SEO signal — and it's what makes link navigation work for screen reader users

We've seen sites improve their organic traffic by **15-25%** after accessibility remediation, with no SEO-specific changes made. The accessibility fixes were the SEO fixes.

Google has never explicitly said "accessibility is a ranking factor." But the overlap between what Google rewards and what accessibility requires is so substantial that the distinction is largely academic. If your site is accessible, it's also well-structured, semantic, fast, and properly marked up — which is exactly what Google wants to see.

## Customer Satisfaction and Retention

Accessibility improvements don't just help disabled users — they improve the experience for everyone. This is often called the "curb cut effect," named after the pavement ramps originally designed for wheelchair users that turned out to benefit parents with pushchairs, delivery workers with trolleys, and travellers with suitcases.

**The digital equivalents are everywhere:**

- **Captions on videos** — Originally for deaf users. Now used by 80% of people who watch video with the sound off, whether they're on a train, in a waiting room, or watching in bed while their partner sleeps
- **High-contrast text** — Originally for people with low vision. Also essential for anyone reading on a phone in bright sunlight
- **Keyboard navigation** — Originally for people who can't use a mouse. Also used by power users, people with temporary injuries (broken arm, RSI), and anyone whose trackpad just broke
- **Clear form labels** — Originally for screen reader users. Also the difference between a form that converts and one that confuses
- **Readable font sizes** — Originally for people with visual impairments. Also appreciated by literally anyone over 40

The Disability:IN 2020 report found that companies leading in disability inclusion had **28% higher revenue**, **2x the net income**, and **30% higher profit margins** than the average in their peer group. While this isn't attributable to web accessibility alone, it reflects the broader principle: businesses that design for the widest possible audience tend to outperform those that don't.

## Brand Perception and Trust

This is harder to quantify but no less real. How a business treats accessibility signals something about its values — and consumers are paying attention.

**Positive signals:**
- An accessibility statement on your website tells visitors you've thought about inclusion — and you're accountable for it
- Accessible design communicates attention to detail and quality. If a company can't be bothered to add alt text to their images, what else are they cutting corners on?
- Inclusive brands build loyalty. Disabled customers who find a site that actually works for them tend to be fiercely loyal — because the bar is so low that any business that clears it stands out

**Negative signals:**
- An inaccessible website tells disabled visitors — and their families, friends, and colleagues — that you don't consider them worth designing for
- Accessibility lawsuits generate press coverage. Being publicly identified as a business that excludes disabled people is a brand risk that no amount of marketing can quickly undo
- The disability community is connected and vocal. A bad experience on your site gets shared in forums, social media groups, and advocacy channels

A Purple survey found that **83% of disabled consumers** limit their spending to businesses they know are accessible. That's not a small, disconnected group — it's a community that actively researches and shares information about which businesses are worth their money.

## Making the Case Internally

If you're reading this because you need to convince someone else in your organisation — a board member, a finance director, a CEO who thinks accessibility is a "nice to have" — here's the summary you can take to them:

### The One-Page Business Case

**The market:** 1.3 billion people globally, $13 trillion in spending power. The UK's Purple Pound alone is worth $309 billion. 69% of disabled consumers leave inaccessible sites.

**The legal risk:** 4,600+ lawsuits in the US in 2024. Average settlement $25,000-$75,000. EU and UK regulations expanding enforcement to private sector. Proactive compliance costs a fraction of reactive defence.

**The SEO impact:** 0.73 correlation between accessibility and SEO scores across 5,000 audits. Sites see 15-25% organic traffic increases after accessibility improvements — with no SEO-specific work.

**The ROI:** 10-25% conversion rate improvements after accessibility fixes. 28% higher revenue for disability-inclusive companies. Remediation costs $2,000-$10,000; a single lawsuit costs $25,000-$100,000+.

**The risk of inaction:** Increasing regulation, increasing litigation, increasing consumer expectation, and an increasingly connected disability community that shares experiences widely.

## My Take

I've spent years making the business case for accessibility, and here's what I've learned: the data is compelling, but it's rarely the data that changes minds. What changes minds is seeing their own website through the eyes of someone who can't use it.

If you've never tried navigating your site with a screen reader, do it once. Put your mouse in a drawer and try to complete a purchase using only your keyboard. Try reading your smallest text with your eyes half-closed. That twenty-minute exercise will do more to build urgency than any ROI spreadsheet.

But if the spreadsheet is what you need — the data is here. Accessibility isn't a cost. It's revenue, risk mitigation, search performance, customer satisfaction, and brand trust, all compounding together. The businesses that understand this aren't doing accessibility because they have to. They're doing it because it makes them money.
