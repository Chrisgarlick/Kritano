# How to Use Website Audits in Your Agency Sales Process

Have you ever walked into a prospect meeting armed with nothing but a generic capabilities deck and a vague promise that you'll "improve their online presence"? I have. More times than I'd like to admit. And I can tell you exactly what happens — the prospect nods politely, says they'll think about it, and you never hear from them again.

The problem isn't your skills. It's that you're asking someone to trust you with their website based on nothing but your word. No evidence, no specifics, no demonstration that you actually understand their situation.

Website audits change that dynamic completely. Instead of walking in with a generic pitch, you walk in with data — specific, actionable data about their own website. Issues they didn't know existed. Scores they can benchmark against. Problems you can quantify in terms of lost traffic, legal risk, and customer experience. It's the difference between "I think I can help you" and "here are twelve things wrong with your website right now, and here's exactly how I'd fix them."

## The Audit-First Sales Approach

The concept is simple: before every prospect meeting, audit their website. Present the findings as part of your pitch. Let the data do the selling.

I started doing this a few years ago and it transformed my close rate. Here's the step-by-step process I use.

### Step 1: Audit the Prospect's Website

Run a full audit 24-48 hours before your meeting. This gives you time to review the results and prepare your talking points rather than scrambling through data on the spot.

PagePulser makes this straightforward — enter their URL, run the audit, and you've got scores across [accessibility](/blog/web-accessibility-2026-why-websites-still-failing), SEO, security, performance, and content quality in a few minutes. But the tool matters less than the preparation. The point is walking in with specifics.

Review the results and identify the findings that will resonate most with this particular prospect. A law firm will care about [accessibility compliance](/blog/understanding-website-accessibility-scores) risk. An e-commerce site will care about [page speed](/blog/core-web-vitals-plain-english-guide) and conversion impact. A B2B company will care about SEO visibility.

### Step 2: Prepare Your Presentation

You don't need to present every finding. In fact, you shouldn't — it's overwhelming and makes you look like you're trying to scare them into signing. Instead, pick **3-5 key findings** that are:

- **Easy to understand** — no jargon, no WCAG criteria codes, no HTTP status codes
- **Clearly impactful** — directly affects their users, their revenue, or their legal exposure
- **Fixable** — you can realistically offer to resolve them

Three well-chosen findings with clear business impact are infinitely more persuasive than a thirty-page report dumped on the table.

### Step 3: Lead With Value in the Meeting

Here's the opening that works almost every time:

*"Before our meeting, I took a look at your website. I found some things I think you should know about — regardless of whether we end up working together."*

That last phrase is important. It signals that you're leading with value, not a sales trick. You genuinely want them to know about these issues, even if they hire someone else to fix them. That builds trust instantly.

Then walk through your 3-5 findings using the audit dashboard or PDF report as a visual. Let them see the scores, the severity labels, and the specific pages affected.

### Step 4: Connect Every Finding to Business Impact

This is where most technically-minded people fall down. You know what a missing `<label>` element means. Your prospect doesn't, and more importantly, they don't care about the technical detail — they care about what it costs them.

**Translate every finding into business language:**

| Technical Finding | Business Translation |
|---|---|
| Missing [alt text](/blog/complete-guide-image-alt-text) on 89% of images | "Search engines can't see your images, and neither can the 2.2 billion people worldwide with vision impairments. You're invisible in image search and potentially non-compliant with accessibility law." |
| Colour contrast failures | "Some of your text is genuinely difficult to read — especially for older visitors or anyone on a phone in bright sunlight. That's friction that drives people away." |
| Missing [security headers](/blog/complete-guide-website-security-headers) | "Your site is missing standard security protections. It's like having a front door with no lock — you might never have a problem, but the risk is completely avoidable." |
| 8-second page load time | "Your site takes 8 seconds to load. Research shows 53% of mobile users leave if it takes more than 3 seconds. You could be losing half your traffic before they've seen a single word." |
| No HTTPS redirect | "Anyone on public wifi could potentially intercept data your visitors submit through your forms. And Google marks your site as 'Not Secure' — which isn't the first impression you want." |

Notice how each one connects to something the prospect already cares about: customers, revenue, risk, or reputation.

## Using PDF Reports as Leave-Behinds

This is a small thing that makes a surprisingly big difference. At the end of the meeting, hand them (or email them) the full PDF report. Here's why it works:

- **It's tangible.** Long after your pitch deck is forgotten, that branded report with their actual scores sits on their desk or in their inbox
- **It's shareable.** The prospect rarely makes the decision alone. They need to take something to their boss, their board, or their business partner. A professional PDF report with documented issues and severity ratings gives them the ammunition to make the case internally
- **It establishes a baseline.** Those scores become the "before" against which your "after" will be measured. It frames the engagement as measurable from day one
- **It demonstrates competence.** You've already shown more initiative and insight in a single meeting than most agencies do in an entire proposal process

## Structuring Your Services Around Audit Results

Here's where the audit-first approach really pays off — it gives you a natural framework for scoping and pricing your services, and the prospect already understands why each tier exists because they've seen the data.

### Quick Fix Package

Address all **Critical and Serious** issues from the audit. These are the barriers that actively cost the prospect traffic, customers, or compliance. Typically 2-4 hours of work.

Position this as a low-commitment, immediate-value engagement. The prospect gets tangible results quickly, you demonstrate your competence on real work, and it opens the door to a larger relationship. It's far easier to sell a 4-hour quick fix than a 40-hour website overhaul — and once you've delivered results, the overhaul sells itself.

### Comprehensive Package

Fix **all issues** across all severity levels — critical, serious, moderate, and minor. Typically 8-16 hours depending on the site's size and complexity.

Position this as a complete website health overhaul. The prospect goes from a failing score to a strong one across accessibility, security, performance, and SEO. It's a bigger commitment, but the audit data makes it easy to justify — every hour of work maps directly to documented issues.

### Ongoing Monitoring Retainer

This is the real prize. **Monthly audits** with a fixed number of fix hours per month. Position it as ongoing website health management — catching new issues as they appear, preventing the site from drifting back into poor health, and providing monthly reports that demonstrate continuous value.

The beauty of this model is that it's self-justifying. Every monthly audit either confirms the site is healthy (which the client values) or surfaces new issues to fix (which keeps the retainer relevant). Either way, the client sees regular proof that you're actively looking after their digital presence.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

I've refined this approach over quite a few prospect meetings, and these are the pitfalls I'd warn you about:

**Don't overwhelm them.** Presenting 47 findings in a single meeting doesn't make you look thorough — it makes the prospect feel like their website is a disaster and the problem is too big to tackle. Start with the high-impact items and let them ask for more detail if they want it.

**Don't be adversarial.** You're not there to shame them for having a bad website. You're there to help. Frame findings as opportunities, not failures. "Here's something that could make a real difference" lands better than "here's everything you've done wrong."

**Don't oversell the urgency.** Yes, some findings are genuinely urgent — an expired SSL certificate, a critical security vulnerability. But inflating the severity of minor issues to create panic undermines your credibility. Be honest about what matters and what can wait.

**Don't forget to listen.** The audit is your opening, not your entire pitch. Once you've presented the findings, ask questions. What are their priorities? What's their budget? What have they tried before? The audit opens the conversation — it shouldn't dominate it.

## My Take

The audit-first approach works because it flips the traditional agency dynamic. Instead of asking the prospect to trust you, you've already demonstrated value before asking for anything in return. You've shown that you understand their specific situation, not just your own capabilities.

I've seen agencies go from a 20% close rate to well over 50% simply by leading with data instead of promises. The audit becomes the proof that you know what you're talking about — and the natural starting point for a conversation about what to fix and how.
